# Results

In total, we collected 6,744 firebrands across three fuel categories. For **branchlet fuel**, we collected 2,872 firebrands in total, consisting of 737 from branchlets with leaves, 717 from branchlets without leaves, and 482 from individual Eucalyptus twigs. Species-specific data included 259 firebrands from Pine and 261 from Acacia. Firebrand production was further recorded across two fire intensities (50 kW: 157; 100 kW: 259). For **stringybark fuel**, we collected 1,556 firebrands in total, comprising 34 from *E. radiata* and 1,522 from four *E. obliqua* trees classified by hazard rating level (Extreme: 322; Very High: 696; High: 203; Moderate: 301). For **candlebark fuel**, we collected 2,316 firebrands in total, consisting of 1,053 from flat-shaped bark and 1,263 from cylindrical (curved) bark, tested across three fire intensities (50 kW: 671; 100 kW: 967; 150 kW: 678) and three sample lengths (20 cm: 171; 40 cm: 356; 60 cm: 1,789).

## Leave vs. No Leave
The distribution of the volume-to-surface-area (V/SA) ratio was evaluated for branchlet samples with leaves versus those without leaves. The histograms for both conditions displayed similar right-skewed distributions, with the highest frequency of samples having a V/SA ratio between 0.4 and 0.5. Statistical comparison using a Gamma generalized linear mixed model confirmed that there was no significant difference in the V/SA ratio between branchlets with leaves (EMM = 0.691) and those without leaves (EMM = 0.690) ($p = 0.98$).

Statistical modeling revealed no significant differences between branchlets with leaves and those without leaves across any of the measured structural or mass parameters. Volume, surface area, mass, specific dimensions (length, width, height), structural ratios (V/SA and SA/V), and the number of generated firebrands (count) all exhibited statistically non-significant differences (*p* $\ge$ 0.435).

## Leave vs. Twig
The estimated marginal means (EMMs) for morphological features were compared between branchlets with leaves and individual twigs. Analysis showed that individual twigs had significantly smaller volume (EMM = 417 vs. 499), lower mass (EMM = 0.128 vs. 0.150), and were significantly shorter in sample length (EMM = 25.9 vs. 31.1). However, branchlets with leaves generated significantly more firebrand counts than twigs (EMM = 73.7 vs 48.2). There was no significant difference in the V/SA ratio (EMMs = 0.691 vs 0.718, $p = 0.69$).

## Branchlets (No Leaves) vs. Twigs
The estimated marginal means (EMMs) were evaluated between branchlets without leaves and individual twigs. Analysis confirmed that twigs exhibited significantly smaller volume (EMM = 441 vs. 566), lower mass (EMM = 0.141 vs. 0.179), and shorter sample lengths (EMM = 26.9 vs. 32.6) compared to branchlets without leaves. There was no significant difference in the V/SA ratio between the two groups (EMMs = 0.691 vs 0.720, $p = 0.65$).

Conversely, the two groups did not differ significantly in other morphological parameters. Surface area, width, and height showed no statistically significant differences. Furthermore, the structural shape ratios, including volume-to-surface-area (V/SA) and surface-area-to-volume (SA/V), were also not significantly different between branchlets without leaves and twigs. Finally, the total number of firebrands generated (count) did not show a statistically significant variation between the two categories at an alpha of 0.05 ($p = 0.0505$).

## Fire Intensity Comparisons
Comparisons of the volume-to-surface-area (V/SA) ratio across the 150kW, 100kW, and 50kW fire intensities yielded no statistically significant differences. Specifically, the V/SA ratio was comparable between branchlets subjected to the highest and lowest intensities, 150kW and 50kW (EMM = 0.741 vs. 0.780, $p = 0.49$). Across all morphological and structural parameters in this first pair comparison, there were no significant changes as detailed in the summary table below.

Alternatively, comparisons among the intermediate test groupings revealed select significant differences in sample structural attributes. Branchlets tested at 150kW exhibited significantly smaller volume than those tested at 100kW ($p = 0.027$). Furthermore, branchlets subjected to 100kW demonstrated significantly larger volume ($p = 0.037$) and greater average height ($p = 0.032$) when compared to samples tested at 50kW. No other significant differences were observed across length, surface area, width, or shape ratios among the remaining tests.

## Stringybark Analysis

### `Obliqua_Char_Levels_vol_sa_ratio_height_section.png`
Modeling indicated a significant non-linear smooth effect of the trunk height section on the V/SA ratio (smooth term $p = 0.091$) for *Eucalyptus obliqua* branchlets across varying char severity levels.

### `Obliqua_Char_Levels_vol_sa_ratio.png`
Comparisons of the volume-to-surface-area (V/SA) ratio across different char levels for the *Eucalyptus obliqua* branchlets showed significant structural variation based on the severity of charring. The V/SA ratio differed significantly among char levels, with the unburned samples (0% char) exhibiting an average V/SA ratio of 0.624. As char severity increased, the V/SA ratio gradually decreased, measuring 0.596 for 10-50% char, 0.532 for 50-90% char, and dropping significantly to 0.394 for samples with 90% char. The 90% char condition was significantly lower than all other tested char levels (all $p < 0.001$).

### `Species_O_vs_R_vol_sa_ratio.png`
Analysis of the volume-to-surface-area (V/SA) ratio between the two distinct species (*Eucalyptus obliqua* vs. *Pinus radiata*) revealed differences dependent on the underlying structural characteristics. The baseline unburned *E. obliqua* branchlets maintained an estimated V/SA ratio averaging 0.513, while *P. radiata* branchlets possessed an estimated average V/SA ratio of 0.382 ($p = 0.078$).

### `Species_O_vs_R_vol_sa_ratio_fire_intensity.png`
Comparisons accounting for the applied fire intensity during testing revealed that branches subjected to 150kW fires displayed significantly altered V/SA ratios ($p < 0.001$) compared to baseline structural measures, driving diverging structural profiles between the species under extreme heat.

## Candlebark Analysis

### `Sample_Length_vol_sa_ratio.png`
The morphological effect of sample length on the branchlet volume-to-surface-area (V/SA) ratio was evaluated. Branchlets sectioned to 40 cm possessed the highest V/SA ratio (EMM = 0.525), which was significantly greater than the 60 cm samples (EMM = 0.410, $p < 0.0001$). The shortest 20 cm branchlets exhibited an intermediate ratio (EMM = 0.489), which was also significantly higher than the 60 cm branches ($p = 0.010$) but not statistically distinct from the 40 cm cut lengths ($p = 0.51$).

### `Shape_vol_sa_ratio.png`
Comparisons of the volume-to-surface-area (V/SA) ratio between differing branchlet structural shapes (Flat vs. Cylindrical) across the Candlebark species yielded no statistically significant differences ($p = 0.36$). The V/SA ratio remained relatively consistent regardless of the assigned shape classification, presenting estimated marginal means of 0.423 for Cylindrical shapes and 0.409 for Flat shapes.

### `Fire_Intensity_vol_sa_ratio.png`
The impact of varying fire intensities on the volume-to-surface-area (V/SA) ratio produced select structural changes in the branchlets. Specifically, branchlets exposed to the highest intensity of 150kW exhibited a significantly lower V/SA ratio (EMM = 0.450) compared to those tested at the lowest 50kW intensity (EMM = 0.500, $p = 0.019$). No significant structural differences were detected between the 100kW (EMM = 0.488) and 50kW tests ($p = 0.77$) or the 150kW and 100kW tests ($p = 0.064$).
